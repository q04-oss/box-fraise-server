Vision

Box Fraise is building an identity network rooted in showing up.

Most sign-in systems accept anyone with an email address. That's cheap to fake, and it leaves every company using them treating each user as unknown by default. We think identity works better when it's earned in person — with a signature from a device only you hold, and a private conversation with a trained Box Fraise consultant who signs off with their own reputation attached.

The consultation is unhurried. The consultant asks the questions; the user doesn't fill in a form. No government ID is checked or stored — we're building our own credential, not verifying somebody else's. What's kept about a user is minimal and human. What the user walks out with is a physical card, the artifact of that specific conversation.

Once a user is verified with us, that status becomes something other products can trust. We open it as a login API — one in-person moment, wider trust downstream.

We're seeding the community through hair. Hair events, hair salons, eventually the software that runs them. Hair is a licensed trade with real in-person craft; stylists are already professionals at private consultation. Same discipline the identity system depends on.

Our events are socialising first — a boy band audition, an opening night, something worth showing up for on its own terms. The consultations happen alongside, in a quieter room, at the pace of a real conversation. What draws people in is the front of the room. What turns them into verified members is the back.

Once two verified members have met in person and want to keep talking, they can chat privately over fraise.chat. We don't treat text as the place where real conversation happens. Text is where you schedule the next dinner.

Dinner clubs are what the network is for. Small groups of six to twelve people, every member having met every other in person, meeting on a cadence they choose. Nothing about the format is our invention: Plato set the Symposium at a drinking party; the French salons, the Bloomsbury Group, and the Inklings all used the same primitive. Adult life outside the university has almost no venue for sustained cross-disciplinary conversation, and Box Fraise is quietly restoring one. New members join a club by being introduced at a dinner in person — the trust a member sat consultation for extends into the group.

Every verified member has a calendar. It's the surface where the rest of the network's rhythms live — salon appointments, event RSVPs, dinner dates, model requests.

We treat that calendar as an education surface as well. Every discipline has a timeline — physics did not fall from the sky in the twentieth century; it was built by named people over centuries in specific places, with specific things left out of the textbook. When a member takes on a subject, its discoveries appear on their calendar in the historical order they happened, walked at whatever pace they choose. What arrives on the day is short: a page, a passage, a demonstration that fits in a study block. Depth is a knob — the same entry can render as "Newton, 1687" or as the fuller picture, including who was not in the room and whose parallel work got erased. The member controls the depth.

The point of walking those timelines is not certification. There are no grades, no credentials, no tests. The point is conversation. Someone who has walked a physics timeline and a hair-biology timeline and a colonial-history timeline can talk to five other people around a table differently than any of them could talk to a specialist. That conversation happens at the dinner. What comes out of the dinner — a new connection, a correction, a proposed addition to a timeline — feeds back into what the next member walks.

The first event is on the calendar. A pilot for the software system will follow when it's ready. What comes after that is what the members make of it.
