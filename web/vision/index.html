Vision

Box Fraise is a hair salon and the software that runs it. The two are inseparable: the salon is where the software gets adopted, and the software is what lets the salon operate at scale.

The salon serves the legal community. Lawyers, judges, clerks, and legal researchers share a professional obligation to a classic appearance that has to be maintained. Box Fraise is built for that clientele. The software is a scheduling system, and clients adopt it the same way they adopt the salon — by walking in for a consultation. Becoming a commercial client is that simple: book a consultation in person at the salon, and the software follows.

The argument. The salon has two claims it makes wherever it can make them — the two speaking points the entire project is built to amplify. First: reasonable doubt in a court of law is a jurisprudential technology that depends on the judiciary being able to imagine the defendant's life. When homelessness has produced defendants whose existence is unimaginable to the bench, the courtroom ceases to be a court and becomes a conviction machine. Second: as long as destitution remains the fallback, workers cannot walk away from exploitation — eliminating homelessness raises the floor beneath every wage negotiation in the country. These are the two points Box Fraise repeats: at its events, in its advertising, through the fund, and at the chairs the legal community sits in every day. Every other move the project makes exists to carry those claims into rooms where they can produce change.

Witness. Ending homelessness is one half of the salon's purpose. The other half is bearing witness to a mental health crisis inside the legal system itself. Democracy cannot be performed when the judiciary is suffering silently. Naming both crises at the same time — the top of society and the bottom — is a claim no other institution in the country is currently prepared to stage. Together the demand and the witness make something the culture doesn't yet have.

Inside the salon is a private legal research lab called Rajzyngier Research. The lab studies legal infrastructure — identity, consent, trust — with the salon as the physical anchor where the research meets the professionals it concerns.

The Legal Justice Fund. Donation-based. It pays members of the legal community to speak at events held by Box Fraise, bringing legal thought into a room where it isn't only heard by other lawyers. It pays for the homeless community to get haircuts every Sunday, at the same chairs the legal community sits in every other day of the week. And it contributes to running the project itself — the coordination and space that make those programs possible.

Aesthetic. The visual identity of the salon is Lady Justice, staged deliberately against the patriarchal representation of justice in contemporary society. Two axes are held in tension: the legal community made to sit alongside the poor whose lives they adjudicate; the female representation of justice made to confront the male apparatus that has claimed the concept. The salon is not neutral about either. Placing the highest and lowest strata of society in the same chair is itself a philosophical move from Rajzyngier Research — proof the lab thinks across the whole stratification of the polis, not only its top.

The boy band. Box Fraise runs a boy band as a form of advertising for the salon. Fans of the band become clients of the salon because the band is the natural cultural extension of the same world. It is how the salon absorbs people who wouldn't otherwise walk through the door of a place that mostly serves legal professionals.

Portraits. Box Fraise photographs its clients and licenses those portraits to businesses with which the client has no prior connection. This is a new form of advertising: the face in the campaign belongs to a real, verified person from the network — chosen deliberately, unaffiliated with the brand — which gives the ad an authenticity that stock photography and celebrity endorsement cannot produce. The person portrayed shares in the fee. The business, having paid for the connection, becomes a candidate to join Box Fraise's in-person events — deepening the link into the network the portrait first opened. Client earnings can be spent on salon services: at other salons at first, at Box Fraise salons as the network grows.

A benefit worth demanding. Box Fraise argues that a monthly haircut with a blowout should be part of every working person's benefits package, alongside health coverage and dental. Regular grooming isn't a luxury; it's a professional expectation, and paying for it out of pocket is a subsidy the employee is quietly giving the employer's image. Employers should cover it. If you work for someone else, demand it.

Scale. Educating the public about homelessness moves the political conversation from identity to class. Box Fraise grows through an entire country and then into multiple countries so it can never be reduced to the kind of insular local business a bully can quietly abuse. A network of salons across countries can look critically at any single region, including its own. It can also look, from safely far away, at another region — using international presence to bear witness when a country's own people are being abused and its neighbours would rather not notice.

The argument is the reason. The witness is the discipline. The salon is the anchor. The software is the fabric. Everything else — the fund, the aesthetic, the band, the portraits, the ask, the network — follows.
