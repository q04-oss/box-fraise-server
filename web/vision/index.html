Vision

Box Fraise is a hair salon and the software that runs it. The two are inseparable: the salon is where the software gets adopted, and the software is what lets the salon operate at scale.

The salon serves the legal community. Lawyers, judges, clerks, and legal researchers share a professional obligation to a classic appearance that has to be maintained. Box Fraise is built for that clientele. The software is a scheduling system, and clients adopt it the same way they adopt the salon — by walking in for a consultation. Becoming a commercial client is that simple: book a consultation in person at the salon, and the software follows.

Inside the salon is a private legal research lab called Rajzyngier Research. The lab studies legal infrastructure — identity, consent, trust — with the salon as the physical anchor where the research meets the professionals it concerns.

The Legal Justice Fund. Donation-based. It pays members of the legal community to speak at events held by Box Fraise, bringing legal thought into a room where it isn't only heard by other lawyers. It pays for the homeless community to get haircuts every Sunday, at the same chairs the legal community sits in every other day of the week. And it contributes to running the project itself — the coordination and space that make those programs possible.

Aesthetic. The visual identity of the salon is Lady Justice, staged deliberately against the patriarchal representation of justice in contemporary society. Two axes are held in tension: the legal community made to sit alongside the poor whose lives they adjudicate; the female representation of justice made to confront the male apparatus that has claimed the concept. The salon is not neutral about either.

The boy band. Box Fraise runs a boy band as a form of advertising for the salon. Fans of the band become clients of the salon because the band is the natural cultural extension of the same world. It is how the salon absorbs people who wouldn't otherwise walk through the door of a place that mostly serves legal professionals.

Scale. Educating the public about homelessness moves the political conversation from identity to class. Box Fraise grows through an entire country and then into multiple countries so it can never be reduced to the kind of insular local business a bully can quietly abuse. A network of salons across countries can look critically at any single region, including its own. It can also look, from safely far away, at another region — using international presence to bear witness when a country's own people are being abused and its neighbours would rather not notice.

The salon is the anchor. The software is the fabric. Everything else — the fund, the aesthetic, the band, the network — follows.
