Rajzyngier Research is a private legal research lab. It studies legal infrastructure — identity, consent, trust, and the physical systems those depend on — with a working preference for research that lands as apparatus rather than pure theory.

Two questions organise the lab's work.

01What is justice?

02What would it take to change society from one where parents can disown their kids into one where kids can disown their parents?

The lab's current working project is Box Fraise — a hair salon and the software that runs it. Every part of Box Fraise is the lab's attempt to answer those two questions in physical form: the salon, the events, the Legal Justice Fund, the portrait program, the software.

Current inquiry

Death rituals of the state

What do a society's death rituals for its state agents reveal about its underlying conception of justice?

This inquiry treats the funeral as a form of political ritual. It examines how societies mark the deaths of those who act in the state's name — police, soldiers, officials — and reads those rituals as encoded statements about legitimacy, sacrifice, authority, and who is granted public mourning. The project holds open the possibility that such rituals serve genuine communal grief as much as they reinforce state power, and treats that tension as central rather than incidental. The aim is analysis, not advocacy: the question is allowed to return an answer the researcher did not predict.

Sub-questions.

  • How does a society decide whose death receives public ritual, and whose does not? (The absence of ritual is as revealing as its presence.)
  • What is asserted when a ritual emphasizes the office over the person who held it?
  • How do the differences between state agents' funerals — soldier, officer, official — map onto what a society claims to value?
  • What does the ritual accomplish for the living: grief, the reaffirmation of authority, or both at once?

Grounding. The inquiry builds on the sociology of ritual and death and on theories of the state's symbolic life: Durkheim on ritual as social cohesion; Robert Bellah on civil religion; Ernst Kantorowicz on the office as distinct from the person who occupies it; Philippe Ariès and Tony Walter on changing attitudes toward death; and existing scholarship on state and police funerals as political ritual.

Counter-position. A serious version of this work must contend with the possibility that these rituals are sincere communal grief rather than encoded ideology — that political meaning is being read into what is, for the mourners, simply loss. The inquiry takes this seriously and asks how grief and ideology can coexist within the same ritual, rather than dismissing either.
