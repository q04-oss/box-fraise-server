A method

Roasted over real fire.

Charcoal roasting is what coffee looked like before the electric drum. Roasters in early Japan built their kilns around binchōtan — a hardwood charcoal that burns hotter, cleaner, and slower than anything a Western roaster had access to at the time. The coffee that came out of those kilns tasted different because the fire was different: less mechanical, more attentive.

That tradition never really left Japan. It also never really arrived in Canada. Most of the coffee you drink here is roasted in gas-fired drums built in Germany or the Netherlands — good machines, precise machines, but machines whose job is to remove the roaster's decisions from the process. Consistency is the point.

We're doing the opposite. In Edson, in a workshop that used to be a mechanic's garage, we roast one batch at a time over a bed of charcoal. The roaster decides when it's done. The flavour of the fire is part of the flavour of the coffee.

"The flavour of the fire is part of the flavour of the coffee. That is what a machine cannot do."

The coffees you drink from us are single-origin because that's the register where the method has the most to say. A washed Mexican and a natural Ethiopian, roasted the same way over the same charcoal, taste as different as they should — the roast doesn't flatten them, it deepens what's already there.

Every bag is roasted the week you order it. We roast on Mondays. We ship on Tuesdays. If you order Wednesday morning, your coffee is in the mail seven days later, no earlier. Coffee is best in the first two weeks off the roast — we'd rather you drink it fresh than have a warehouse full of stale bags.

The shop in Edson is open Thursday through Sunday. Come sit for a cup, watch a batch come off the fire, take home a bag someone made this morning.
