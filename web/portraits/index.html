Portraits

American Apparel used their own employees and customers in every ad for a decade. No models, no celebrities. The people in the frame embodied a specific aesthetic — a view of what the brand stood for — and viewers could feel it.

Box Fraise portraits do the same for a different vision. Each client is photographed as an embodiment of justice — the aesthetic and philosophical stance the salon is built around. Licensing the portrait brings that stance into your brand.

The platform takes a cut. The rest goes to the person portrayed.

The directory. Businesses that want to participate join a public directory printed on the landing page. Signup is by email — no self-serve form. Whoever is running the salon reads the note, vets the business, and adds it by hand.

How a business gets a portrait. Every Box Fraise event opens with a set of societal questions the room discusses together. After the discussion the floor pivots business by business. For each business in the directory, everyone in attendance is asked, one at a time, whether they'd like to model for that company — and why, or why not. When more than one attendee wants to, they debate each other in front of the room. Speaking skills develop alongside the vote. The debate itself — positive or negative — becomes marketing content the business can carry beyond the portrait.

Business owners are welcome to attend. If the feedback is a compliment, they can bank it. If it's a critique, they can respond in front of the same room the critique was voiced in. Either way, the exchange plants the business in the memory of every attendee — many of whom become clients of that business by way of having heard the founder speak.

Beyond portraits. Businesses that keep participating deepen their relationship with the project. First the portrait license. Then attendance. Then a consultation. Then adopting Box Fraise's scheduling system on their own operations. Then the point-of-sale system. Each step is a transaction the platform participates in — clients, marketing, software, hardware — and each step draws the business further into an ecosystem Box Fraise is quietly building around the salon.

Eventually, businesses that stay deepest in the network become co-owners of the physical spaces the network operates in.

Reviews and their resolution. Once a business is operating on Box Fraise software, its clients can leave reviews. Positive ones publish. Negative ones enter a resolution window: before publishing, the business owner has time to show up at a Box Fraise event and answer the reviewer in front of the room. If they show up, the review stays private. If they don't, it publishes. That reverses the standard shape of online reviews. Anonymous complaint met with defensive PR becomes face-to-face accountability under witness. A new standard for reviews, and the marketing that follows.

The portraits are how you meet us. What comes after is what the project is actually building.
